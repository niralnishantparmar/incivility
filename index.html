Essential Question  ·  History Strand A2

"How have economic, political and cultural decisions promoted or prevented the growth of personal freedom, individual responsibility, equality and respect for human dignity?"

Our Answer

By 1859 to 1860, the United States had exhausted every legislative compromise on slavery. Economic decisions, specifically the Southern economy's total dependence on enslaved labor, made any real compromise impossible: slaveholders could not afford to accept limits on slavery, and the North could not afford to keep subsidizing a system that violated the founding ideals of human dignity.

John Brown's raid on Harper's Ferry in October 1859 was a direct consequence of political failure. Decades of compromises, from the Missouri Compromise to the Kansas-Nebraska Act, had only delayed the reckoning. Brown believed that legal and political channels had been exhausted and that only violent action could force the nation to confront slavery. His raid failed militarily, but it succeeded in making the sectional crisis impossible to ignore.

Jefferson Davis's Senate Resolutions in February 1860 represent the political and cultural counterpart. Rather than moving toward equality and human dignity, Davis demanded that the federal government actively protect the institution of slavery. He framed slaveholding as a constitutional right, placing property rights above the basic human dignity of millions of enslaved people. This was a direct political decision that prevented any growth of personal freedom.

Together, these events show how economic dependence on slavery drove political extremism, which in turn shattered cultural trust between the North and South. The result was a nation that could not hold together. The Civil War was not an accident: it was the direct consequence of decades of economic, political, and cultural decisions that prioritized the profits of a few over the freedom and dignity of millions.

Context: America on the Brink, 1850 to 1859

Political Context

A Nation Splitting Apart

By the mid-1850s, the United States was fracturing along sectional lines. The Compromise of 1850, the Fugitive Slave Act, the Kansas-Nebraska Act of 1854, and "Bleeding Kansas" had exhausted goodwill between North and South. The Republican Party, founded in 1854 on a platform opposing slavery's expansion, rose rapidly. Moderate voices were losing ground to extremists on both sides.

Economic Context

Slavery and the Southern Economy

The Southern economy was built almost entirely on enslaved labor. By 1860, the South's enslaved population represented the largest single financial asset in the entire United States, worth more than all the nation's railroads and factories combined. Southern elites genuinely believed their civilization could not survive without slavery, making any compromise on the issue existential rather than political.

✦   Event One   ✦

The Raid on Harper's Ferry (October 1859)

Background

Who Was John Brown?

John Brown (1800 to 1859) was a radical abolitionist from Ohio who believed from an early age that God had called him to destroy slavery. Unlike most abolitionists, who favored peaceful persuasion, Brown was convinced that violence was the only effective weapon. After the Pottawatomie Massacre of 1856, in which Brown and his sons killed five pro-slavery settlers in Kansas, he became a polarizing national figure: a hero to radical abolitionists and a murderous fanatic to the South and many Northern moderates.

Background

The Plan

By 1857 to 1859, Brown was raising money and recruiting supporters for a far more ambitious scheme. He planned to seize the federal armory at Harper's Ferry, Virginia, which held over 100,000 weapons. Armed with these weapons, he expected enslaved people throughout the South to rise up in rebellion, flee to the mountains, and fight for their freedom. The plan was secretly funded by a group of wealthy Northern abolitionists known as the "Secret Six."

Description

The Night of October 16, 1859

On the night of October 16, 1859, Brown led 21 men (16 white, 5 Black) across the Potomac River into Harper's Ferry. They quickly seized the federal armory and the adjacent engine house, cut telegraph lines to prevent word from spreading, and took several prominent local citizens as hostages, including a great-grandnephew of George Washington. Brown fully expected enslaved people to flood into Harper's Ferry once word spread. They did not come. Local militiamen and armed civilians began surrounding the armory on October 17, exchanging fire with Brown's small force. Several of Brown's men were killed, and Brown retreated into the engine house with his remaining men and hostages.

Description

The Marines Arrive

On October 18, President Buchanan dispatched a force of U.S. Marines under the command of Colonel Robert E. Lee and Lieutenant J.E.B. Stuart. Lee offered terms; Brown refused. Marines stormed the engine house within minutes. Ten of Brown's men were killed in the fighting, including two of his sons. Brown himself was wounded, captured, and taken into custody. The entire raid lasted less than 36 hours.

Description

Trial and Execution

Brown was swiftly tried in Charlestown, Virginia, on charges of treason against the state of Virginia, murder, and conspiracy with enslaved people. He was convicted on all counts. During his trial, Brown delivered calm, eloquent speeches that transformed him in Northern eyes from a violent fanatic into a principled martyr. He was hanged on December 2, 1859. On the morning of his execution, he handed a guard his now-famous final note: "I, John Brown, am now quite certain that the crimes of this guilty land will never be purged away but with blood."

"I, John Brown, am now quite certain that the crimes of this guilty land will never be purged away but with blood."

John Brown, final written note, December 2, 1859

Significance in American History: The Harper's Ferry raid was a turning point that made sectional reconciliation nearly impossible. The South was horrified not only by the attack itself, but by the sympathy many Northerners expressed for Brown after his execution. Church bells rang in many Northern towns at the moment of his hanging. Southern leaders concluded that Northern public opinion had turned against them. The raid confirmed every Southern fear about Northern abolitionism, strengthened the hand of Southern fire-eaters who called for secession, and made the 1860 election a zero-sum contest with no room for compromise. Even Robert E. Lee, who put down the raid as a federal officer, would resign his commission 18 months later to fight against that same government.

Key Figures: Harper's Ferry

John Brown

Radical abolitionist. Led the raid. Executed December 2, 1859. Became a Northern martyr and a Southern symbol of Northern aggression.

Col. Robert E. Lee

Commanded U.S. Marines who retook the armory. Celebrated as a federal hero in 1859; resigned to command Confederate forces in 1861.

Frederick Douglass

Prominent abolitionist who knew Brown but did not join the raid, fearing it would fail. Later called Brown "the man who lived by the sword."

✦   Event Two   ✦

The Jefferson Davis Resolutions (February 1860)

Background

The South in Early 1860

By early 1860, Southern Democrats felt their position was desperate. Several Northern states had passed "Personal Liberty Laws" that effectively nullified the Fugitive Slave Act of 1850, refusing to help return escaped enslaved people. The Republican Party was surging in the North and was widely expected to win the 1860 presidential election on a platform explicitly opposing slavery's expansion into new territories. Southern leaders feared that without federal protection of slavery, the institution would eventually be surrounded, isolated, and destroyed.

Background

Jefferson Davis's Strategy

Jefferson Davis of Mississippi was one of the most powerful men in the U.S. Senate. A former secretary of war and military hero, he was the leading voice of Southern orthodoxy on slavery: it was not a necessary evil, as older Southerners had argued, but a positive good, constitutionally protected and morally justified. Davis's strategy in early 1860 was to force Northern Democrats to either accept a set of pro-slavery resolutions or face the dissolution of the Democratic Party, the last truly national institution binding North and South.

Description

The Four Resolutions

On February 2, 1860, Davis introduced four resolutions in the U.S. Senate. First: neither Congress nor a territorial legislature could abolish or restrict slavery in any territory. Second: Congress was constitutionally obligated to protect slaveholders' property rights in all territories, if local governments failed to do so. Third: no state could legally obstruct the return of fugitive enslaved people. Fourth: any attack on slavery, by any entity, was an unconstitutional attack on the rights and equality of the Southern states. The resolutions were not modest requests. They were a demand that the federal government become an active enforcer and protector of slavery nationwide.

"I hold that in the Union we have rights which are guaranteed to us by the Constitution, and that the denial of those rights is itself a dissolution of the bond which held us together."

Jefferson Davis, 1860

Description

The Senate Showdown

Northern Democrat Stephen Douglas of Illinois refused to accept the resolutions. Douglas had built his career on "Popular Sovereignty," the idea that territorial settlers, not Congress, should decide the slavery question. Davis's resolutions would have required Congress to impose slavery on unwilling territories, destroying Douglas's position entirely. Douglas knew that accepting the resolutions would cost him every Northern state in the 1860 election. Davis knew that Douglas's refusal would destroy the Democratic Party. Both men were right.

Description

The Party Splits

At the 1860 Democratic National Convention in Charleston, South Carolina, the party fractured catastrophically. Southern delegates, unwilling to nominate Douglas, walked out. The convention reconvened in Baltimore; Southern delegates walked out again. Two separate Democratic conventions nominated two separate candidates: Douglas for the North, John C. Breckinridge of Kentucky for the South. A Constitutional Union candidate, John Bell, entered the race as well. The divided opposition vote handed the presidency to Abraham Lincoln, who did not even appear on the ballot in most Southern states.

Significance in American History: The Jefferson Davis Resolutions were the final legislative attempt by Southern leadership to force the United States government to actively protect and expand slavery. The failure to achieve this goal, and the Democratic Party split that followed, guaranteed Lincoln's election. Lincoln's election triggered South Carolina's secession on December 20, 1860, followed by six more states within weeks. Jefferson Davis, who had introduced the resolutions as a U.S. Senator, was inaugurated as President of the Confederate States of America on February 18, 1861. The Civil War began on April 12, 1861, less than 14 months after Davis introduced his resolutions.

Key Figures: Davis Resolutions

Jefferson Davis

U.S. Senator from Mississippi. Introduced the resolutions. Would become President of the Confederate States of America in 1861.

Abraham Lincoln

Republican presidential candidate in 1860. Won the election thanks to the Democratic Party split, triggering secession and the Civil War.

Harriet Tubman

Abolitionist and conductor on the Underground Railroad. Her work rescuing enslaved people represented exactly what Davis's resolutions sought to prevent.

02

The Jefferson Davis Resolutions

February 2, 1860  ·  United States Senate, Washington D.C.

Key Figure

Who Was Jefferson Davis?

  • U.S. Senator from Mississippi and former Secretary of War
  • Champion of states' rights and the protection of slavery
  • Military hero of the Mexican-American War
  • Believed slavery was a positive good, not just a necessary evil
  • By 1860, convinced the South must take a stand or lose everything

Context

The South in 1860: Under Siege

  • Northern states passing laws blocking the Fugitive Slave Act
  • Republican Party expected to win the 1860 presidential election
  • Republicans opposed ALL expansion of slavery into new territories
  • After Harper's Ferry, the South felt the North wanted to destroy them
  • Southern Democrats decided to force a confrontation NOW

The Setting

The Senate Floor, February 2, 1860

  • Davis rose to address the U.S. Senate
  • The chamber was the last arena where North and South still debated
  • Tensions ran extremely high after Harper's Ferry
  • This was not a request. It was an ultimatum.
  • Accept these terms, or the Democratic Party is over

The Four Resolutions

Davis's Four Demands

  • (1) Congress CANNOT restrict slavery in any territory
  • (2) Congress MUST protect slaveholders' property in all territories
  • (3) No state can block the return of escaped enslaved people
  • (4) Any attack on slavery is an unconstitutional attack on Southern rights
  • Directed at Northern Democrats: accept this or the party is finished

The Fallout

The Democratic Party Splits

  • Northern Democrat Stephen Douglas refused to accept the resolutions
  • At the 1860 convention in Charleston, Southern delegates walked out
  • Two separate Democratic candidates were nominated
  • Douglas for the North; Breckinridge for the South
  • Split vote handed the presidency to Abraham Lincoln

The Consequence

Lincoln Wins, the South Secedes

  • November 1860: Lincoln elected with only Northern votes
  • Lincoln did not appear on the ballot in most Southern states
  • South Carolina seceded December 20, 1860
  • Six more states followed within weeks
  • The Civil War began April 12, 1861

The Aftermath

From Senator to President of the Confederacy

  • Davis resigned from the U.S. Senate in January 1861
  • Inaugurated as Confederate President, February 18, 1861
  • His resolutions had set the chain of events in motion
  • The man who demanded federal protection of slavery now led its armed defense
  • The Civil War lasted 4 years and killed 620,000 Americans

Significance in American History

  • Final legislative attempt to force federal protection of slavery
  • Destroyed the last national institution holding the country together: the Democratic Party
  • Made Abraham Lincoln's election inevitable
  • Lincoln's election triggered secession; secession triggered war
  • Davis went from introducing resolutions to commanding the Confederacy in under 12 months
